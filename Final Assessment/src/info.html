Destinations in COX’S BAZAR

Cox's Bazar, located in southeastern Bangladesh, boasts the world's longest natural sea beach, stretching over 120 kilometers along the Bay of Bengal. Famous for its stunning sandy shores and vibrant local culture, it's a popular tourist destination offering picturesque sunsets and a range of water-based activities. The town's unique blend of natural beauty and coastal charm makes it a captivating getaway.

History of Cox's BazarA Tapestry of Dynasties and Colonization

The rich history of Cox's Bazar, located in southeastern Bangladesh, weaves a captivating tale of shifting powers and cultural influences over the centuries. In the early 9th century, the greater Chittagong area, including Cox's Bazar, found itself under the dominion of Arakan kings until its eventual subjugation by the Mughals in 1666. Notably, Mughal Prince Shah Shuja was enraptured by the area's scenic allure during his journey to Arakan, leading his forces to camp amidst its picturesque landscape. The name "Dulahazara," meaning "one thousand palanquins," echoes this historical episode, resonating with the prince's grand retinue. Following the Mughal era, Cox's Bazar witnessed a succession of rulers, from the Tipras and Arakanese to the Portuguese and eventually the British. The appellation "Cox's Bazar" was etched into history through the endeavors of Captain Hiram Cox, an officer of the British East India Company. Appointed as the Superintendent of Palonki outpost, Cox undertook the task of resettling Arakanese refugees in the area. Although he passed away in 1799 before completing his mission, his legacy endured through the establishment of a market named in his honor: Cox's Bazar. This market would lay the foundation for the town's development, formally established in 1854 and subsequently gaining municipal status in 1869. The mid-19th century brought pivotal shifts as the Indian Subcontinent grappled with the aftermath of the Sepoy Mutiny. The British East India Company faced mounting criticism, prompting its dissolution on January 1, 1874. In this transformative period, the assets of the company, including its armed forces, transitioned to the British Crown. Cox's Bazar was designated a district within the Bengal Province under this new regime, marking another chapter in the town's enduring history. Cox's Bazar stands as a testament to the intricate interplay of cultures, rulers, and events that have shaped its narrative. From the grandeur of Mughal royalty to the resilience of resettled refugees and the echoes of colonial endeavors, the town's history is an evocative tapestry that continues to unfold against the backdrop of its stunning coastal beauty.
